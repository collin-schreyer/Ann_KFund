# State Department Export Control Policy Guidelines

## 1. Overview

These guidelines establish policy for State Department personnel reviewing export license applications and determining compliance with ITAR and related regulations.

## 2. Classification Determination

### 2.1 Initial Review

When reviewing an item for export control classification:

(a) Determine if the item has military application
(b) Check if item appears on USML (ITAR) or CCL (EAR)
(c) Assess whether item is specifically designed or modified for military use
(d) Consider dual-use applications

### 2.2 Jurisdiction

**ITAR Jurisdiction (State Department):**
- Items specifically designed, developed, configured, adapted, or modified for military application
- Items listed on the USML
- Defense services related to USML items

**EAR Jurisdiction (Commerce Department):**
- Commercial items
- Dual-use items not on USML
- Items with predominant civil applications

## 3. Technology Transfer Controls

### 3.1 Technical Data Release

Technical data release to foreign persons requires authorization when:

(a) Data relates to USML items
(b) Data is not in the public domain
(c) Recipient is a foreign person (non-U.S. citizen/permanent resident)

### 3.2 Deemed Export

A deemed export occurs when:
- Technical data is released to a foreign person in the United States
- Foreign person has access to controlled technology or source code
- Training is provided on USML items to foreign persons

## 4. License Application Review Criteria

### 4.1 Approval Factors

Consider the following when reviewing license applications:

(a) **End-User:** Is the end-user a government, military, or commercial entity?
(b) **End-Use:** What is the stated purpose? Is it consistent with the item?
(c) **Destination:** What are the foreign policy concerns for this country?
(d) **Proliferation Risk:** Could this contribute to WMD or destabilizing military capabilities?
(e) **Human Rights:** Does the destination country have human rights concerns?

### 4.2 Red Flags

Indicators that warrant additional scrutiny:

- Vague or unclear end-use statements
- End-user reluctant to provide information
- Delivery to freight forwarder or intermediary
- Requested route inconsistent with destination
- Item incompatible with stated end-use
- Packaging inconsistent with stated method of shipment

## 5. Country-Specific Policies

### 5.1 NATO Allies

Generally favorable consideration for:
- Items supporting interoperability
- Items for joint operations
- Standard military equipment

### 5.2 Major Non-NATO Allies

Case-by-case review with consideration for:
- Bilateral defense relationships
- Regional stability impact
- Technology security concerns

### 5.3 Embargoed Countries

Presumption of denial for:
- Countries under UN arms embargo
- Countries designated as State Sponsors of Terrorism
- Countries subject to U.S. sanctions

## 6. Compliance Verification

### 6.1 Post-Shipment Verification

May be required for:
- Significant military equipment
- Sensitive technology transfers
- First-time end-users
- High-risk destinations

### 6.2 Blue Lantern Program

End-use monitoring program that conducts:
- Pre-license checks
- Post-shipment verifications
- Unfavorable information inquiries

## 7. Exemptions and Exceptions

### 7.1 Canadian Exemption

Certain exports to Canada are exempt from licensing requirements, except:

- Items in USML Category I (firearms)
- Items for re-export to third countries
- Items subject to UN embargoes

### 7.2 NATO Exemption

Limited exemptions for classified military information shared under NATO agreements.

## 8. Recordkeeping Requirements

Exporters must maintain records for 5 years:

- Export license applications and authorizations
- Shipping documents
- End-use certificates
- Correspondence with foreign parties
- Technical data transfer documentation
